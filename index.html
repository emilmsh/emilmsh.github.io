Emil Halseth

Senior Economist, Oslo Economics

I am a Senior Economist at Oslo Economics, where I work on competition economics and empirical market analysis. My research is in industrial organization: how firms compete with each other in markets, with a particular focus on the grocery retail sector, using quasi-experimental methods, structural econometrics, and surveys.

Before joining Oslo Economics I was a PhD research scholar at the Department of Economics, NHH Norwegian School of Economics, where I am completing my PhD in economics. My work has been published in The Scandinavian Journal of Economics, where it was awarded the prize for the best article published in 2024.

Publications

  • “The Effect of Cross-Border Shopping on Commodity Tax Revenue: Results from Norway's COVID-19 Border Closings” with Richard Friberg, Frode Steen, and Simen A. Ulsaker The Scandinavian Journal of Economics, 126(4), 733–772, 2024 — awarded the journal's prize for the best article published in 2024 · full text at publisher (open access)
    Abstract

    We use grocery data from Norway and COVID-19 border closings to gauge the effect of cross-border shopping on commodity tax revenue. Detailed store-category level data identify differential treatment effects that depend on distance to Swedish stores. Economically significant effects extend to up to two hours' drive from the border, and even further for prominent cross-border shopping products as beer, cigarettes and soda. Across all products, cross-border shopping decreases tax revenue from VAT by 3.6% at the national level. National commodity tax revenue from carbonated soft drinks (subject to a sugar tax) is reduced by 8.1% and from cigarettes by 11.9%.

Working Papers

  • “The Competitive Effects of Store-Format Repositioning: Evidence from a Norwegian Grocery Acquisition” Under journal review — paper (PDF) · online appendix (PDF)
    Abstract

    This paper evaluates Coop's 2015 acquisition of ICA, which raised national concentration in Norwegian grocery retail and reshaped local competition through store-format repositioning toward soft-discount. Store-level difference-in-differences estimates for a leading soft-discount incumbent imply that rival conversions to the acquirer's soft-discount format reduced sales by 4.4–12.9%, while closures increased them by 2.4–11.1%. Convenience-discount conversions show no sales response. Local product prices were unchanged, consistent with uniform national pricing, and assortments adjusted little. Nationally, food prices fell 3.8–5.2% relative to neighboring countries. These findings suggest the merger reallocated complementary firm-specific assets—ICA's locations and Coop's soft-discount concept—strengthening competition despite greater concentration.

  • “Do Informed Consumers Pay Less? Evidence from a Survey with Linked Grocery Purchase Data” with Richard Friberg, Frode Steen, and Simen A. Ulsaker paper (PDF)
    Abstract

    We examine how consumer price knowledge affects prices paid in grocery markets, combining survey-based price recall data from over 2,000 Norwegian households with 18 months of linked transaction histories. Better-informed consumers pay lower prices by timing purchases to coincide with sales: a 10 percentage-point increase in price knowledge is associated with a 1.3 percent reduction in prices paid. This provides direct empirical support for the central mechanism in Varian's (1980) model of sales. Consumers who actively seek price information have higher price knowledge, suggesting that policies improving price accessibility may improve consumers' ability to identify and act on temporary discounts.

Ongoing Projects

  • Local Competition and Market Structure in Norwegian Grocery Retail with Frode Steen and Simen A. Ulsaker · project lead Supported by a research grant from the Norwegian Competition Authority (Konkurransetilsynet), 2026–2028.
    Project outline

    How does the structure of local grocery markets — the number of stores and their distribution across chains — affect competition, cost efficiency, and store accessibility? The Norwegian grocery sector is highly concentrated at the national level, yet competition largely plays out in local markets, and the policy debate has centred on measures that shape local market structure: the 2024 ban on negative servitudes, proposals to cap the number of stores per chain in a local market, and the Competition Authority's new market-investigation powers, which open for structural remedies such as divestiture. This project develops and estimates a structural equilibrium model of store entry and local competition between grocery chains, building on recent frameworks in empirical industrial organization and detailed data on store locations, chain affiliation, and local market conditions. The estimated model is then used for counterfactual analyses that quantify how concrete policy interventions would affect entry patterns, market structure, competitive intensity, and consumer welfare — explicitly accounting for the chains' strategic responses.
