Emil Halseth

Senior Economist, Oslo Economics

I am a Senior Economist at Oslo Economics, where I work on competition economics and empirical market analysis. My research is in industrial organization: how firms compete with each other in markets, with a particular focus on the grocery retail sector, using quasi-experimental methods, structural econometrics, and surveys.

I joined Oslo Economics in 2025 after six years as a PhD research scholar at the Department of Economics, NHH Norwegian School of Economics, where I am completing my PhD in economics. My work has been published in The Scandinavian Journal of Economics, where it was awarded the prize for the best article published in 2024.

Publications

  • “The Effect of Cross-Border Shopping on Commodity Tax Revenue: Results from Norway's COVID-19 Border Closings” with Richard Friberg, Frode Steen, and Simen A. Ulsaker The Scandinavian Journal of Economics, 126(4), 733–772, 2024 — awarded the journal's prize for the best article published in 2024 · publisher · working paper (SSRN)
    Abstract

    We use grocery data from Norway and COVID-19 border closings to gauge the effect of cross-border shopping on commodity tax revenue. Detailed store-category level data identify differential treatment effects that depend on distance to Swedish stores. Economically significant effects extend to up to two hours' drive from the border, and even further for prominent cross-border shopping products as beer, cigarettes and soda. Across all products, cross-border shopping decreases tax revenue from VAT by 3.6% at the national level. National commodity tax revenue from carbonated soft drinks (subject to a sugar tax) is reduced by 8.1% and from cigarettes by 11.9%.

Working papers

  • “The Competitive Effects of Store-Format Repositioning: Evidence from a Norwegian Grocery Acquisition” Under journal review — paper (PDF) · online appendix (PDF)
    Abstract

    This paper evaluates Coop's 2015 acquisition of ICA, which raised national concentration in Norwegian grocery retail and reshaped local competition through store-format repositioning toward soft-discount. Store-level difference-in-differences estimates for a leading soft-discount incumbent imply that rival conversions to the acquirer's soft-discount format reduced sales by 4.4–12.9%, while closures increased them by 2.4–11.1%. Convenience-discount conversions show no sales response. Local product prices were unchanged, consistent with uniform national pricing, and assortments adjusted little. Nationally, food prices fell 3.8–5.2% relative to neighboring countries. These findings suggest the merger reallocated complementary firm-specific assets—ICA's locations and Coop's soft-discount concept—strengthening competition despite greater concentration.

  • “Do Informed Consumers Pay Less? Evidence from a Survey with Linked Grocery Purchase Data” with Richard Friberg, Frode Steen, and Simen A. Ulsaker
    Abstract

    Do more informed consumers pay lower prices? Using an online survey, we measure consumers' knowledge of grocery prices and link their responses to 18 months of individual-level purchasing history from Norway's largest grocery retail group — more than 2,000 households and over 70,000 recalled prices across two chains and 24 products. Consumers with higher price knowledge systematically pay lower prices — not because they buy inherently cheaper products or shop at inherently cheaper stores, but because they time purchases to coincide with temporary sales. Moving from relatively low to relatively high price knowledge is associated with a 4 percentage-point decrease in the average price paid, and the returns are larger for price-conscious consumers, for baskets with volatile prices, and for storable products. Price knowledge itself is best explained by product characteristics and observed shopping behaviour, and consumers who actively gather price information know more — suggesting that policies improving the accessibility of price information can strengthen competition.
